Methodology.

We ranked 40 health metrics by ROI — the signal strength of each measurement weighed against what it costs and whether knowing the number actually changes what you do. Here's how.

Three scoring axes.

Every metric was evaluated on three dimensions. A metric has to score well on all three to make the foundation tier. High evidence but unactionable? Tier 3. Cheap but low signal? Doesn't make the cut.

AXIS 01
Causal evidence strength
How strong is the evidence that this metric causally predicts health outcomes? We prioritize randomized controlled trials and Mendelian randomization over observational cohorts, and observational cohorts over cross-sectional studies. Association is not causation — and most health tracking is built on association.
AXIS 02
Cost to measure
What does it actually cost to collect this data? A $40 blood pressure cuff that lasts 5 years costs $8/year. Your phone counting steps costs nothing. A DEXA scan costs $200 per data point. Cost per measurement, not cost per device.
AXIS 03
Actionability
Does knowing this number change what you should do? Blood pressure: yes, immediately and specifically. Cortisol from a single draw: almost never — the normal range is too wide and too context-dependent. This axis is where most "health optimization" tracking falls apart.

Evidence hierarchy.

Not all studies are equal. A 1-million-participant meta-analysis of RCTs carries more weight than a 500-person cross-sectional survey. When we cite evidence for a metric's ranking, here's how we weight it:

1 Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) + meta-analyses
2 Mendelian randomization studies
3 Prospective cohort studies (10K+ participants)
4 Smaller cohort / retrospective studies
5 Cross-sectional / expert consensus

Every Tier 1 metric is backed by at least one large RCT or Mendelian randomization study. Most are backed by multiple.

Where more data causes harm.

Baseline's credibility depends on not over-medicalizing healthy people. We intentionally exclude or down-weight metrics where the expected value of testing is negative.

×
PSA / CA-125 (cancer markers). Population screening leads to overdiagnosis cascades. PSA: ~27 men overtreated for every 1 death prevented. CA-125 definitively does not reduce ovarian cancer mortality (UKCTOCS 2021, 200K women).
×
CGMs for non-diabetics. No evidence that glucose variability in healthy people has clinical significance. Documented increase in food anxiety and orthorexic tendencies.
×
Cortisol (single draw). Wide normal range, affected by sleep, stress, time of day, caffeine. Creates false certainty from a single number. Diurnal curves are $200–400 and still hard to interpret.
×
Proprietary composite scores. Oura "Readiness," Whoop "Recovery" — opaque algorithms, not peer-reviewed, not clinically validated. Track the underlying data (HRV, RHR, sleep) instead.
×
Detailed calorie/macro tracking. 30–50% underestimation even when actively logging (Lichtman, NEJM 1992). Associated with disordered eating in susceptible individuals. Pattern-level is enough.

Scoring design principles.

These principles guide how coverage scores are weighted:

1. Foundation first. A person with blood pressure, lipids+ApoB, metabolic panel, family history, and Lp(a) has more health insight than someone with 10 wearable metrics but no blood work.

2. One-time data has disproportionate value. Family history and Lp(a) are collected once and permanently change your risk model. The scoring system strongly incentivizes these.

3. Wearable data is context, not core. Sleep, steps, RHR, and HRV make blood work actionable — they correlate habits with biomarker changes. High-ROI because they're passive, but they don't score above core clinical data.

4. Actionability gates inclusion. If knowing a metric doesn't change what you should do, it gets weighted lower regardless of predictive power. Homocysteine is a good example: strong predictor, but lowering it hasn't improved outcomes.

5. Never recommend things that can harm. The coverage score should never push someone toward a test where the expected value is negative.
