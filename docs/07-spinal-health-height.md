# Baseline — Spinal Health & Height Preservation
**Date:** February 25, 2026 | **Purpose:** Cross-cutting recommendation layer — connects resistance training, Vitamin D, sleep, waist-to-height ratio, and height measurement

This document emerged from walking through the waist-to-height ratio and a question about whether axial loading (squats/deadlifts) compresses the spine. The answer inverts the common intuition and connects multiple metrics in the scoring engine.

---

## The Core Insight

Heavy axial loading (squats, deadlifts) is **net protective** for spinal health. The fear that "heavy weights compress the spine" confuses an acute, transient biomechanical event (3-5mm fluid expression per session, recovers in hours) with chronic degenerative aging (disc dehydration, bone loss, muscle atrophy over decades). The chronic adaptation to loading is the opposite: denser bone, better-hydrated discs, stronger paraspinal muscles.

**The person who squats heavy twice a week will, on average, be measurably taller at age 70 than their sedentary peer.** (Content hook — counterintuitive enough to share.)

---

## What Causes Height Loss

Average lifetime loss: **0.8-2.0 inches (men)**, **1.2-3.0 inches (women)**. Accelerates after 50; women lose more due to post-menopausal bone density decline.

| Factor | Contribution | Modifiable? | Onset |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disc dehydration/thinning** | 33-50% | Partially | 30s, accelerates 50s |
| **Vertebral compression fractures** | 20-30% | Yes (preventable) | 55 (F), 65 (M) |
| **Thoracic kyphosis** (postural rounding) | 15-25% | Yes (partially reversible) | 40s |
| **Paraspinal muscle atrophy** | 10-15% | Yes | 40s |
| **Foot arch collapse + joint cartilage** | ~5% | Partially | 50s |

The single biggest *acute* cause is vertebral compression fractures — one fracture = 5-15mm of permanent height loss. 25% of postmenopausal women have at least one by age 70 (Melton et al., JBMR 1989). Many are silent — no pain, just height loss.

## Intervention Ranking

### Tier 1: Strong Evidence, Large Effect

**1. Progressive resistance training with axial loading (squats, deadlifts)**
- Targets: bone density, disc health, paraspinal muscle, posture — 4 of 5 factors simultaneously
- Howe et al. 2011 (Cochrane, 43 RCTs, n=4,320): +1-2% lumbar BMD/year vs -1%/year in controls
- Watson et al. 2019 (LIFTMOR trial, n=101 postmenopausal women with low bone mass): heavy deadlifts and squats significantly improved lumbar spine BMD. Zero adverse events.
- Granhed et al. 1987 (n=25 powerlifters): significantly higher vertebral bone mineral content than controls
- Belavy et al. 2016 (n=79): axial loading associated with better disc hydration (MRI T2 signal) and larger disc cross-sectional area — discs *hypertrophy* with load
- Sinaki et al. 2002 (10-year follow-up, n=50): back extensor exercises → 1.6% vs 4.3% vertebral fracture rate

**2. Adequate calcium + Vitamin D**
- Tang et al. 2007 (Lancet, 29 RCTs, n=63,897): ~12% fracture risk reduction
- Dose: 1200mg calcium + 800-2000 IU vitamin D daily
- Connects to Tier 2 #17 in scoring engine (Vitamin D)

**3. Adequate sleep (7-9 hours recumbent)**
- The *primary* disc rehydration mechanism. Discs recover 15-19mm of compression nightly via osmotic rehydration
- No decompression practice (hanging, inversion, swimming) comes close
- Connects to Tier 1 #5 in scoring engine (Sleep)

### Tier 2: Moderate Evidence, Moderate Effect

**4. Targeted spinal extensor exercises**
- Bansal et al. 2014: reduced kyphosis 3-5 degrees in 12 weeks (≈5-10mm recovered height)
- Sinaki: back extensor strength was the single best predictor of fracture risk 10 years later
- Note: Paul's back mobility stack for Kasane is adjacent to this

**5. Smoking cessation**
- Battie et al. 1991 (Twin Spine Study, 115 identical twin pairs): smoking accelerates disc degeneration
- Connects to smoking risk modifier in scoring engine

### Tier 3: Weak Evidence

**6. Decompression practices**

| Practice | Acute height recovery | Long-term preservation | Bone density | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hanging | Yes (minutes, reverses) | No evidence | None | Low |
| Inversion tables | Yes (minutes, reverses) | No evidence | None | Low |
| Swimming | N/A | No evidence | **Negative** (removes gravity) | Low |
| Yoga | Modest (posture only) | Possible (posture) | Minimal | Low-Moderate |

Swimming is notable: good for cardiovascular health, bad for bone density because it removes gravitational loading. Swimmers have lower BMD than runners or lifters (Fehling et al., JBMR 1995).

## Acute vs. Chronic: Why Squats Don't Shrink You

| Timeframe | What happens | Reversible? | Clinical significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single session | 3-5mm compression (fluid expression from discs) | Yes, within hours | None — same mechanism as diurnal height loss from standing |
| Weeks-months | Disc and vertebral adaptation, increased bone density | N/A (positive) | Protective |
| Decades (moderate training) | Maintained or improved disc hydration + bone density | N/A | Strongly protective |
| Decades (extreme overload, poor recovery) | Possible accelerated disc changes | Partially | Low concern, outweighed by benefits |

The relevant comparison isn't "heavy squats vs. no loading." It's "heavy squats vs. the alternative" — and the alternative (sedentary aging) produces dramatically worse spinal outcomes.

## What "Optimized" Looks Like (Scenario Comparison)

| Scenario | Men lifetime loss | Women lifetime loss |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary, poor nutrition | 1.2-2.0 in (3-5 cm) | 2.0-3.0 in (5-7.5 cm) |
| Basic exercise + nutrition | 0.8-1.2 in (2-3 cm) | 1.2-2.0 in (3-5 cm) |
| Optimized (heavy resistance, targeted extensors, Ca+D, sleep, bone monitoring) | 0.4-0.8 in (1-2 cm) | 0.8-1.2 in (2-3 cm) |

Gap between worst and best case: ~1-2 inches. That's the ROI of the full intervention stack.
